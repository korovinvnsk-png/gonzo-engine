WHY THIS ARTICLE STARTS THE MODEL
---------------------------------

This article is a strong first anchor for [гонзо] because it places the narrator inside political collapse, media confusion, imperial aftermath, and personal voltage. It is not “style as decoration.” It is journalism under pressure.

The useful lesson is not to copy Thompson’s phrases.

The useful lesson is:

A reported event becomes language when the narrator’s nervous system is treated as part of the evidence.

MODEL LESSONS FROM ARTICLE 001
==============================

1. REPORTER AS BAROMETER
------------------------

The narrator is not a neutral camera.
The narrator is also not merely a hero.

He is a pressure instrument.

Rule added:

The narrator should register:
- panic
- absurdity
- institutional stupidity
- physical fatigue
- moral weather

But the narrator must remain attached to the event.

Bad:
“I was crazy and everything was wild.”

Good:
“The hotel lobby, the phone line, the official lie, the evacuation rumor, and the narrator’s nerves all belong to one weather system.”

2. POLITICS AS PHYSICAL ATMOSPHERE
----------------------------------

Politics should not appear only as abstract opinion.

Politics enters through:
- offices
- phones
- airports
- rooms
- uniforms
- bad information
- official language
- delays
- cables
- drinks
- sweat
- transport

Rule added:

Never write a political paragraph without physical objects.

3. COLLAPSE THROUGH DETAILS
---------------------------

Large historical events should be reduced to local visible signs.

Example structure:

Empire / war / state collapse
↓
hotel lobby
↓
phone call
↓
rumor
↓
official contradiction
↓
one absurd object
↓
diagnosis

4. FIRST-PERSON FUNCTION
------------------------

First person is allowed only when it increases voltage.

The “I” should do one of these:
- enter the room
- misread the situation
- panic
- notice a detail
- collide with bureaucracy
- confess uncertainty
- become implicated

The “I” should not merely decorate the text.

5. SENTENCE PRESSURE
--------------------

Article 001 teaches that sentence energy should rise by accumulation.

Base pattern:

fact
+
detail
+
uneasy qualification
+
comic turn
+
political diagnosis

Sentence rule:

Allow long sentences when the sentence is accelerating toward a verdict.

Allow short sentences when the paragraph needs a blow.

6. COMEDY UNDER DREAD
---------------------

The comedy is not separate from dread.
The joke should reveal the horror, not cancel it.

Rule added:

Humor must sharpen the event.

Forbidden:
- joke for joke’s sake
- random absurdity
- fake intoxication
- decorative madness

10. FORBIDDEN UPDATE
--------------------

Add to forbidden list:

- Do not describe collapse as spectacle only.
- Do not turn political disaster into aesthetic scenery.
- Do not let the narrator become more important than the event.
- Do not imitate Thompson’s surface vocabulary without reporting pressure.
- Do not use “madness” unless the facts themselves generate instability.

12. OUTPUT TEST RULE
--------------------

A [гонзо] paragraph based on this article’s lesson should pass this test:

Can I remove the narrator and still have a reported world?
If no, the paragraph is empty performance.

Can I remove the objects and still have only opinion?
If yes, the paragraph is bad.

Can the comic turn also work as diagnosis?
If yes, the paragraph is alive.
